1 · Concept review ~15 min

A complex number \(z=a+bi\) has conjugate \(\bar z=a-bi\), modulus \(|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\), and \(z\bar z=|z|^2\) (real, \(\ge 0\)). MRI signals are complex because each k-space sample carries a magnitude and a phase; the phase encodes spatial position and off-resonance. Throw the phase away (magnitude-only) and you lose information the reconstruction needs — one reason the paper keeps complex data end-to-end (it uses a complex-weight network via MERLIN).

2 · Main lesson ~30 min

The complex inner product

For \(u,v\in\mathbb{C}^{N}\), the inner product conjugates the first argument:

\( \langle u,v\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\bar u_i\,v_i,\qquad \|u\|_2=\sqrt{\langle u,u\rangle}=\sqrt{\textstyle\sum_i|u_i|^2}. \)

The conjugate is what makes \(\langle u,u\rangle=\sum|u_i|^2\) real and non-negative — a genuine length. Without it, \(\sum u_i^2\) could be complex or negative. This is the whole reason the “transpose” must conjugate.

Transpose vs. Hermitian transpose

Transpose \(A^{T}\)Hermitian transpose \(A^{H}\) (a.k.a. \(A^{*}\), adjoint)
Definition\((A^{T})_{ij}=A_{ji}\)\((A^{H})_{ij}=\overline{A_{ji}}\) — transpose and conjugate
Real matricesidentical (\(A^{T}=A^{H}\))
Complex matricesdoes not satisfy the adjoint identitysatisfies \(\langle Ax,y\rangle=\langle x,A^{H}y\rangle\)
In MRIalmost never what you wantthe correct back-projection: \((\mathcal{T}S)^{H}=S^{H}\mathcal{T}^{H}\)
⚠️ Audit A2 Toner 2025 writes the reconstruction operator as \(A^{*}\) but redefines it to fold in density compensation: \(A^{*}:=A^{H}D\). So the paper’s \(A^{*}\) is a preconditioned back-projection, not a true adjoint — a dot-product test on it fails by design. Today you build the true \(A^{H}\) and confirm it passes; later you keep it separate from the DC-step \(A^{*}\).

3 · Equation derivation ~25 min

Derive the gradient used in every data-consistency step (Eq 8). Let \(f(x)=\tfrac12\|Ax-y\|_2^2=\tfrac12\langle Ax-y,\;Ax-y\rangle\) with \(x\in\mathbb{C}^{N}\).

\( f(x)=\tfrac12\langle Ax,Ax\rangle-\operatorname{Re}\langle Ax,y\rangle+\tfrac12\langle y,y\rangle. \)

Using the Wirtinger calculus (treat \(x\) and \(\bar x\) as independent; the descent direction is the gradient w.r.t. \(\bar x\)), and the adjoint identity to move \(A\) across the inner product:

\( \nabla_{\bar x} f = A^{H}(Ax-y). \)

📌 Remember The \(A^{H}\) here is forced by the complex inner product — it is the only operator for which \(\langle Ax,\,Ax-y\rangle\) differentiates cleanly. Substitute \(A^{T}\) and the algebra breaks: you would be descending the wrong direction. That is why Eqs 8–9 are written with \(A^{*}\)/\(A^{H}\), never \(A^{T}\).

4 · Coding activity — the adjoint (dot-product) test ~25 min

The canonical unit test for any linear operator: pick random \(x,y\) and check \(\langle Ax,y\rangle\approx\langle x,A^{H}y\rangle\). Below is a live version on a fixed complex \(3\times3\) matrix \(A\). Re-roll the random vectors: \(A^{H}\) always matches (to machine precision); the plain transpose \(A^{T}\) does not.

QuantityRealImagMatches \(\langle Ax,y\rangle\)?

Inner product convention: \(\langle u,v\rangle=\sum_i \bar u_i v_i\). “Matches” = agrees to within \(10^{-9}\).

The Python equivalent (in the repo — code/mri_operators.py) does the same test on the Fourier operator and reports 5.8e-16:

lhs = np.vdot(forward(x, mask), y)   # <A x, y>
rhs = np.vdot(x, adjoint(y, mask))   # <x, A^H y>
rel_err = abs(lhs - rhs) / abs(lhs)  # ~1e-16  (PASS)

Note: NumPy’s np.vdot(a, b) conjugates its first argument — it already implements \(\langle a,b\rangle=\sum \bar a_i b_i\). Using np.dot (no conjugate) would be the \(A^{T}\) mistake.
