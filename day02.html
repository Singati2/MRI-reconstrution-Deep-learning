1 · Concept review ~15 min

A matrix \(A\in\mathbb{C}^{m\times n}\) is a linear operator: \(x\mapsto Ax\). Two factorizations tell you what it does. Eigenvalues (\(Av=\lambda v\)) exist for square matrices and describe directions the operator merely scales. Singular values exist for any matrix and describe how much the operator stretches orthonormal input directions into orthonormal output directions.

2 · Main lesson ~30 min

The SVD and low-rank truncation

Every \(A\in\mathbb{C}^{m\times n}\) factors as

\( A = U\Sigma V^{H} = \sum_{k=1}^{r}\sigma_k\,u_k v_k^{H},\qquad \sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\dots\ge\sigma_r>0. \)

The rank-\(P\) truncation \(A_P=\sum_{k=1}^{P}\sigma_k u_k v_k^{H}\) is the best rank-\(P\) approximation in the \(\ell_2\)/Frobenius sense (Eckart–Young). The explained variance of keeping \(P\) components is

\( \text{explained}(P)=\dfrac{\sum_{k=1}^{P}\sigma_k^{2}}{\sum_{k=1}^{r}\sigma_k^{2}}. \)

🟠 MRI The paper builds a dictionary of T2 signal-decay curves (SEPG model, Day 3), SVDs it, and keeps \(P=4\) principal components that hold 99.96% of the variance. Those 4 “temporal basis images” replace the 32 echo-time images as the network’s channels (Eq 4) — this is the temporal-sparsity idea from compressed sensing, reused by deep learning.

The Discrete Fourier Transform

The DFT matrix \(F\) with \(F_{kn}=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{N}}e^{-2\pi i kn/N}\) is unitary (\(F^{H}F=I\)), so by Parseval it preserves energy: \(\|Fx\|_2=\|x\|_2\). In MRI, \(F\) maps image ↔ k-space. The radial acquisition uses a nonuniform FFT \(\mathcal{T}\) (samples off the Cartesian grid), but the unitary intuition still guides the adjoint \(\mathcal{T}^{H}\) you built on Day 1.

⚙️ convention “Centered” FFT = fftshift(fft(ifftshift(x))) with norm="ortho". ifftshift before, fftshift after puts DC at the image centre and makes the transform unitary — the convention used in code/mri_operators.py.

3 · Coding activity — SVD energy & the temporal subspace ~25 min

Below is the real SVD spectrum of a synthetic 24-curve T2-decay dictionary (echo spacing 8.1 ms, 32 echoes, T2 from 20–160 ms), computed with NumPy. Drag the slider to choose how many principal components \(P\) to keep and watch the explained variance. This is exactly the compression in Eq 4.

96.6403% of variance captured  ·  discarding 23 components

This synthetic, noiseless dictionary is even more compressible than the paper’s (P=3 already ≈ 99.99%); Toner 2025 reports 99.96% at P=4 on their real SEPG dictionary. The lesson is the same: signal-decay curves live in a tiny subspace.

# NumPy equivalent (what produced the numbers above)
U, S, Vt = np.linalg.svd(X, full_matrices=False)   # X: 24 curves x 32 echoes
explained = np.cumsum(S**2) / (S**2).sum()          # cumulative variance
P = np.searchsorted(explained, 0.9996) + 1          # smallest P for 99.96%
